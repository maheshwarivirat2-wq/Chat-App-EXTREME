# Non-Developer Startup: (failed before even starting up?)
It's 2026, and the tech world keeps screaming: "Build an app in 10 minutes with Al!" I completely fell for it. My plan was to launch a startup using automated Al workflows. But then I realized I needed a reality check: I had to test my own capabilities first. I needed to know if the tech was actually magical, or just clever marketing. So, I paused the startup daydream and threw myself into the deep end to build my very first solo project. **I'm NOT a developer.** I had no idea how to wire a backend or secure a database. I just wanted to see what happens when a non-coder partners with an Al. **Spoiler alert:** It was a massive roller coaster of brilliant breakthroughs, terrible design choices, and literal database overrides. Here is what actually happened.
## Who should read this, and what to expect
Let's get one thing straight. If you are a pro software engineer looking for perfect, high-level code... Politely close this tab. You will just cringe at my logic anyway. This blog is purely for the non-developers. It's for the people who have great ideas but don't know how to write a single line of code. It's for anyone who keeps seeing those viral videos-"How I built an Al startup in a weekend!"-and wants to know what happens when you actually sit down and try it yourself. If you want to use Al to learn, grow, or just prove you can build a real app from scratch, this is for you. I'm going to share what creating an app actually looks like when your Al assistant sometimes acts like a genius, and other times puts white text on a bright cyan bubble and thinks it's a design masterpiece. Expect some weird bugs, terrible Ul choices, a few moments of total confusion, and the amazing feeling of finally making the thing work.
## How I started- MY Chat APP! (Which nobody asked for) 
Let me be honest. Nobody on this planet needs another chat app. We already have WhatsApp, Instagram, and Discord. I knew from day one that I wasn't building the next big communication tool. So why did I choose to build a chat app? Because I wanted a project that would serve as the ultimate training ground to test my capabilities. Here is the thing about chat apps: on the surface, they look incredibly simple. You type a message, hit send, and a little bubble pops up. How hard could that be, right? Well, there is a massive difference between what you think it takes and what it actually requires. Asking an Al to build a basic visual prototype takes about five minutes. But making it actually work in the real world? That means wrestling with real-time databases, managing user sessions, figuring out how to update colors dynamically without crashing the page, and securing the data so people can't just delete each other's messages.
I didn't want to be the guy who just generates a fake, non-functioning webpage, takes a screenshot, and starts yapping on the internet about how easy coding is now. I wanted to build the real thing. I wanted to hit the actual walls that developers hit, solve the problems, and prove to myself that I could build something with actual purpose and complexity.
## The Procedure: Expectation vs. Reality
Most people think building an app with Al is like ordering fast food. You just type, "Make me a chat app," hit enter, and the Al hands over a perfectly working product on a silver platter. **The reality?** Not even close.
You quickly realize the Al cannot manage the whole backend by itself. It gets confused, forgets what it did five minutes ago, and bugs pop out of nowhere. Suddenly, you are the one who has to sit down, brainstorm, and figure out why the database is crashing. I won't lie-seeing all those red error messages for the first time was genuinely scary. It was such a tiring experience trying to wrap my head around a system I had never seen before. So, does that mean building an app with Al as a non-developer is impossible? No. Obviously not. If it was impossible, how did I manage to build this app? The truth is, creating software is much easier today than it has ever been. But here is the catch: **Al will write the code, but it will not do the thinking for you.** It still requires high-level problem-solving. You have to be the architect. You have to figure out the logic, connect the dots, and guide the Al out of its own messes. You just can't run from the problem-solving part (That's why most people fail).
## The Real Roadblocks
*(Quick warning: If you absolutely hate the gritty, behind-the-scenes details of how apps break, feel free to skip this section. But if you want the juicy drama, grab a seat.)*
Before we get into the chaos, you need to know my setup. I used Codex to write the code, Supabase for my backend and database, and Vercel to deploy the app so it could actually live on the internet. And let me just admit something right now: the user interfaces for GitHub and Supabase looked like alien control panels to me at the start. I was totally lost. I actually had to rely on another Al assistant (shoutout to Gemini) acting as my personal guide just to tell me which buttons to click without accidentally deleting my whole project. Here are the biggest walls I hit, my actual reactions, and how I survived them.
### 1. The Deployment Traps
Early on, I just wanted to see if the app actually worked on the internet. So, I tried to push it to Vercel to check the progress.
 * **What I expected:** A nice loading bar and a live link.
 * **What I got:** A wall of screaming red errors.
Vercel suddenly cared about things that worked perfectly fine on my computer but apparently broke the rules of the internet. It was throwing build errors, complaining about missing packages, and refusing to launch. My reaction was pure annoyance. I just wanted to see my chat app!  I learned very quickly that writing the code is only half the battle; packaging it up so a server accepts it is a whole different game. We had to pause everything, read the error logs, and manually install missing pieces just to get the green light.
### 2. The Timeline File Conflict (The Stuff of Nightmares)
Oh my God, I am still scared of this problem just thinking about it. When you build with Al, you are constantly making changes, testing, and saving. But at one point, the timeline of my files got completely messed up. Codex was trying to update a file, but the version it was looking at was different from the one I had just saved. This led to a massive "file conflict."
Basically, the system was confused about what was the real code. My reaction? **Pure panic.** I thought I had broken the app forever and lost days of work. I literally had to force myself to stop, breathe, and figure out how version control works. I had to learn how to reject broken code, close bad pull requests, and manually revert things back to a safe checkpoint to save my project from collapsing in on itself. And no, AI CAN'T solve this problem :)
### 3. The Database Disconnect (And The Fake Bugs)
This was probably the most frustrating roadblock of all. Here is the secret they don't tell you: your Al (Codex) cannot see your live database. It only sees the old text files in your code folder. So when I needed to fix something in Supabase-like changing user permissions or adding a new column to track when someone read a message-I had to go into the database and do it myself. But because Codex couldn't see my live database, it would constantly report fake bugs. It would stop working and scream, "Error! This column doesn't exist!" And I would be sitting there looking at my screen like, "I hope this stupid error is as stupid as it looks, because I literally just added that column myself." Because of this disconnect, I had to travel into every single database table manually to make sure the backend matched the frontend. If I hadn't stepped in to act as the bridge between the Al and the database, the project would have been completely paralyzed by errors that didn't even exist.
### 4. The Sandbox Trap (Yeah... I Did This on a Mobile)
Yeah, I did it on a mobile. Let that sink in for a second.
While everyone else is sitting in front of three glowing monitors, I was trying to engineer a real-time database, manage routing issues, and fix code bugs inside a cloud Sandbox environment on a tiny screen. And let me tell you, the Sandbox can be an absolute trap. Because I was stuck in a cloud environment, I ran into some ridiculous technical walls. For example, there were times when the Codex CLI (the command-line tool that actually lets you manage the app and push changes) wasn't even installed properly in my workspace. I would try to run a simple command to fix a bug, and the system would just stare back at me with an error.
I have to admit something here: if you are building an app and you do it on a proper desktop, you are going to have a massive edge. On a computer, you can easily integrate Al skills like Figma for your Ul design, manage your Vercel deployments locally without pulling your hair out, and actually look at your code and your terminal at the same time. Now, does having a desktop mean you get complete freedom and a magical bug-free experience? Absolutely not. You are still going to suffer through the exact same database disconnects and timeline conflicts I just talked about. Al will still test your patience. But you will definitely have a serious operational advantage-an edge that I absolutely did not have while fighting a cloud interface.
## Conclusion: The Final Verdict
Alright, brutal honesty time. If you think building an app with Al means typing a magic prompt and waking up with a startup, snap out of it. Though it's possible for simple projects (like an app that just generates random excuses for why you didn't do your homework), But the moment you decide to build something real complicated (which requires heavy backend work, just like my Chat App), You'lll probably end up closing your laptop... or throwing your phone away.
Here is what it actually takes to survive this process:
 * **1. You Can't Outsource Problem-Solving:** Al is a hyper-fast typist, but you are the architect. When the database crashes or the Ul bugs out, the Al will just blindly keep writing bad code. You have to connect the dots, figure out the logic, and guide it out of its own mess.
 * **2. Extreme Patience (The PR Graveyard):** My GitHub right now is a literal graveyard of closed pull requests. There were so many times the Al wrote code that completely shattered the app. You have to get comfortable throwing work in the trash, hitting revert, and starting over without throwing your device against the wall.
 * **3. Getting Used to the Red Screens:** You will see massive red error screens. It will feel like the whole app is on fire. You have to learn to stop panicking, read the error (even if it looks like gibberish), and deal with it.
So, is it worth it? Absolutely. One thousand percent.
When you finally hit merge, open your app, and see a real-time message pop up on a system you engineered from nothing, it is the most empowering feeling in the world. You no longer need a Computer Science degree to build software. You just need grit, logic, and the stubbornness to keep going when the Al inevitably does something stupid. If I can engineer a real-time chat architecture from a mobile phone, you have absolutely zero excuses. Go build something.
